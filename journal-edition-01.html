Editor's Note

The Weight of the World

We spend an inordinate amount of our lives trying to remove friction. We pay premiums for next-day delivery, we utilise algorithms that predict our tastes before we even articulate them, and we interface with the world through smooth, featureless glass rectangles. The overarching promise of the 21st century has been one of seamless integration: a life where intent and execution are practically simultaneous.

Yet, as we curated the pieces for this inaugural edition of the AI Love You Journal, a persistent, counter-intuitive theme emerged. In an era that idolises the effortless, there is a profound, growing hunger for the difficult. We are seeking out the tactile, the analogue, and the heavy. We are looking for things that push back.

This issue, which we have loosely anchored around the concept of "Analogue Precision & Tactile Living," explores this desire. It is an examination of why, when given the option of infinite ease, we sometimes choose the path of deliberate resistance.

You will see this tension in the cinema we explore. Wim Wenders’ Perfect Days and Chantal Akerman’s Jeanne Dielman are profoundly different films, yet both force us to confront the weight of mundane routine. They demand our patience, refusing to offer the quick dopamine hits of modern storytelling, asking instead that we sit with the characters in the quiet, repetitive labour of existence.

You will hear it in the music. We examine the enduring power of the "room sound," tracing the acoustic purism of Talk Talk to the organic textures of The Smile. In an age where digital production can simulate absolute perfection, these artists remind us that true resonance requires a physical space; a room filled with air, wood, and the inevitable imperfections of human performance.

This craving for physical engagement bleeds into our design and domestic spaces. We are spotlighting the resurgence of haptic interfaces (the satisfying click of a mechanical camera dial or a synthesiser button) which serve as a psychological anchor, reminding us that we are still operating within the physical world. We see it in the rise of Tropical Brutalism, where the cold, industrial weight of board-formed concrete is embraced and softened by lush, encroaching nature, creating homes that feel less like machines for living and more like fortified sanctuaries.

Even in our wardrobes, the items that endure (like the classic French chore coat) are those born from utility, heavy fabrics, and hard labour, rejecting the ephemeral nature of fast fashion in favour of clothes that earn their patina through use.

Ultimately, this edition is about intentionality. Whether it is the terrifying, beautiful precision required to execute a perfect pour-over coffee with Japanese hardware, or the commitment required to listen to an entire album recorded in a single room, the subjects of this journal share a common thread: they require us to show up. They ask for our attention, our physical effort, and our time.

"In a world engineered to be weightless, we hope this first edition of the AI Love You Journal provides something satisfyingly heavy to hold onto."

Enjoy the issue.

[I. Movies] Radical Routine

The Quiet Radicalism of Routine

Wim Wenders and Chantal Akerman prove that the most cinematic moments happen when absolutely nothing seems to be happening at all.

There is a moment early in Wim Wenders’ masterful Perfect Days (2023) where Hirayama, a quiet man who cleans public toilets in Tokyo, selects a cassette tape for his morning drive. The click of the tape deck is distinct, satisfying, and heavy with intent. It’s an analogue ritual in a digital city, a tactile anchor in an ephemeral existence. This scene encapsulates the film’s thesis, and indeed, the thesis of a certain, meditative branch of cinema: that the beauty of life is found not in grand dramatic arcs, but in the meticulous, almost sacred performance of daily routine.

Perfect Days is a celebration of "analogue precision." Wenders, moving away from his often sprawling road movies, finds a new kind of kinetic energy in the repetitive actions of his protagonist. We see Hirayama wash his face, water his saplings, buy his coffee from the same vending machine, and clean toilets with artisanal precision. The film forces the viewer to slow down, to appreciate the geometry of a well-cleaned space or the texture of sunlight (the film introduces us to the beautiful Japanese concept of komorebi; the dappled light filtering through tree leaves).

Kōji Yakusho as Hirayama, finding joy in the morning commute in Wim Wenders' Perfect Days (2023). Source: Master Mind / MUBI.

This approach is not without its risks. As veteran film critic Roger Ebert famously noted in his review of another masterpiece of mundane repetition, boredom can be an essential cinematic tool. Ebert was writing about Chantal Akerman’s monumental Jeanne Dielman, 23 quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles (1975). To appreciate Wenders' warmth, we must contrast it with Akerman’s cool, structuralist radicalism.

Delphine Seyrig in the meticulously choreographed kitchen of Jeanne Dielman, 23 quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles (1975). Source: Criterion Collection.

While Wenders finds poetry in routine, Akerman finds prison. For three and a half hours, we watch Jeanne Dielman, a middle-aged widow, perform domestic tasks; cooking potatoes, making the bed, attending to her teenage son. These actions are presented in real-time, uninterrupted. There are no dramatic close-ups, no soundtrack to instruct our emotions. The narrative, as Manohla Dargis wrote for The New York Times, isn't about the events themselves, but about "the terrifying weight of the interval between them."

Akerman used repetition to quantify domestic drudgery and objectification. Jeanne’s routine includes turning tricks for money in the afternoon (another routine task, handled with the same robotic detachment as peeling a potato. The film’s tension arises not from what happens, but from the increasing precision required to maintain this fragile ecosystem. The subtle cracks in her routine) a dropped fork, a moment of hesitation; are more thrilling than any action sequence, precisely because Akerman established the rigidity of the ritual.

Perfect Days and Jeanne Dielman represent two poles of cinematic repetition: one a spiritual refuge, the other a psychological cage. Wenders uses the tactile (the physical sensation of a cassette, the roughness of a sponge) to connect his character to the world. Akerman uses the repetitive to show a character retreating from the world, locked in a cycle of maintenance that eventually, inevitably, shatters.

Both films require a different type of viewing posture. They ask us to stop anticipating the next plot point and instead inhabit the moment. In a world that prizes the "frictionless" experience, Wenders and Akerman remind us that sometimes friction (the precise, difficult, repetitive engagement with the physical world) is where true meaning, and true radicalism, resides.

[II. Music] Acoustic Architecture

The Architecture of Silence

From Talk Talk’s Spirit of Eden to The Smile’s Wall of Eyes, we explore the power of the 'room' sound.

Modern recording spaces, focused on acoustic intimacy to capture the "room sound". Source: Unsplash / Abbey Road Studios.

We live in an age of quantised perfection. Modern music production often seeks the "frictionless" ideal: vocals tuned to mathematical precision, drums locked to an unbreakable digital grid, every frequency polished into a shimmering, sterile sheen. This isn't necessarily a critique; it’s simply the aesthetic of the current algorithm. Yet, there is a counter-movement that craves the imperfect, the resonant, and above all, the haptic; the sensation of sound waves interacting with physical space. This is the enduring appeal of the "room sound."

The room is where music breathes. It’s the slight echo on a snare drum, the sympathetic resonance of an acoustic guitar, the barely audible creak of a musician shifting weight. In an era where music is often built one instrument at a time in a silent virtual environment, hearing the "architecture" of the recording space feels rebellious.

The Smile (comprising Thom Yorke, Jonny Greenwood, and Tom Skinner) leaned heavily into this philosophy on their second album, Wall of Eyes (2024). Recorded largely at Abbey Road Studios (the definitive monument to analogue acoustic design) the album sounds alive. It doesn't sound like a perfect assembly of files; it sounds like three exceptionally talented people figuring out how their instruments converse in a specific chamber.

Yorke’s vocals, often filtered and abstracted in his electronic work, here are raw and immediate. On the title track, you can almost feel the air moving around his microphone. Greenwood’s intricate guitar lines aren't just signals; they are acoustic events. Music writer Sheldon Pearce noted the album's "tangible, wooden texture," highlighting how the band prioritises performance over polish.

The pursuit of organic sound relies heavily on the physical space and heavy analogue equipment. Source: Unsplash / Abbey Road Studios.

But to truly understand the zenith of this acoustic purism, we must look back to 1988 and Talk Talk’s masterpiece, Spirit of Eden. Abandoning the commercial synth-pop that made them famous, the band, led by the late Mark Hollis and producer Tim Friese-Greene, retreated to a darkened, converted church in Suffolk. They spent nearly a year recording improvisational sessions, inviting dozens of guest musicians (from jazz trumpeters to a choir) to play without sheet music, reacting only to the mood.

The resulting sound was not engineered; it was captured. Spirit of Eden is defined not by the notes that are played, but by the space between them; the silence that Hollis famously called "more important than the note." It is an album that demands to be listened to in its physical entirety, as a dynamic, shifting soundscape where instruments emerge from and dissolve into the ambient noise of the room. Critical reception at the time was famously confounded; Q Magazine called it "impenetrable." Today, however, it is universally regarded as a landmark of post-rock, a template for the ambient-organic hybrid sound that bands like The Smile strive for.

Recording the room is difficult. It requires patience, expensive microphones, and an acceptance of imperfection. But the result (the raw vitality of Spirit of Eden, the intricate intimacy of Wall of Eyes) offers something that no digital plugin can simulate: the profound humanity of sound captured in a physical space.

[III. Products] The Dry Goods Aesthetic

Engineering the Perfect Pour

Coffee, for many, is the definitive start-of-day ritual. It is a moment of necessary pause. For a rapidly growing subset of connoisseurs, the "frictionless" automated coffee maker is anathema. They crave process. They crave control. This desire has fuelled the global dominance of Japanese specialty coffee hardware, an industry defined by meticulous engineering disguised as elegant minimalism. This is the "Dry Goods Aesthetic": tools that look better the more they are used, prioritising function whilst achieving a geometric beauty that borders on the architectural.

The Hario V60 ceramic dripper, celebrated for its spiral ribbing and precise extraction dynamics. Source: Hario.

The global specialty coffee renaissance began, arguably, with the Hario V60. First, they used a single, large extraction hole at the bottom. Second, they engineered spiral ribs on the inner walls of the cone. Its geometric design was engineered to create an air gap between the filter paper and the glass, maximizing airflow. "It is a terrifying and beautiful level of analogue control," says specialty champion Kyle Glanville.

If Hario is about dynamic flow, Kalita is about engineered consistency. Their iconic "Kalita Wave" dripper (the 185, for larger batches) takes a different path. Instead of one large hole, the Kalita Wave uses three smaller, precise extraction points at the flat bottom of a metal or ceramic cone. The iconic "wave" filters do not touch the inner walls, creating a large, insulated air gap that maintains temperature stability.

"The Wave is the antithesis of the 'cone'," explains James Hoffmann, author of The World Atlas of Coffee. "The flat bottom creates a uniform bed depth across the entire extraction surface. This makes the Kalita Wave inherently more forgiving and capable of incredibly sweet, clean extractions." For those obsessed with consistency, the Kalita Wave is a masterpiece of passive engineering.

But a precision dripper is useless without a precision pour. Enter the gooseneck kettle, a category mastered by Japanese design studio Kinto. Kinto’s "Slow Coffee Style" collection isn't just hardware; it’s an entire aesthetic ecosystem. Their kettles, defined by a narrow, graceful gooseneck spout, are counterbalanced to perfection. The weight sits low in the hand, allowing for the slow, laminar water flow required for a delicate pour-over, minimising agitation and ensuring even saturation.

Japanese coffee design understands that a true ritual requires tactile satisfaction. These tools are engineered to reward attention. The dry, precise geometry of Hario and Kalita, paired with the ergonomic balance of Kinto, elevates the morning coffee from a chemical necessity to a moment of crafted perfection.

[IV. Design] Haptic Interface

Push Button, Get Joy: The Resurgence of the Haptic Interface

The tactile satisfaction of mechanical camera dials governing the exposure. Image: Unsplash. Source: Leica Camera.

We are drowning in a sea of smooth, glowing glass. Our phones, our tablets, our car dashboards, and increasingly, our home appliances have surrendered their physical controls to the touchscreen. The interface has become frictionless, adaptive, and overwhelmingly efficient. And yet, many of us feel an abstract sense of loss. We miss the definite click of a well-engineered switch, the creamy resistance of a weighted dial, and the tactile feedback of a physical keyboard. We miss the haptic; the sensation of touch as a primary method of navigation and satisfaction.

This nostalgia isn't just Luddism; it’s a design correction. High-end product design is experiencing a renaissance of the tactile, recognising that true utility often requires a physical anchor. We are learning that friction (the good kind) is what makes us feel connected to our tools.

Leica cameras are perhaps the ultimate example of this tactile resistance. In a market dominated by electronic screens and menus, a Leica M11 still features a physical, mechanical shutter speed dial, an ISO dial, and a physical aperture ring on its lenses. You "set" the camera with definitive, clicking actions.

"With a touchscreen, you are operating in a simulated environment," says Alice Rawsthorn, acclaimed design critic and author of Design as an Attitude. "With a Leica, you are directly manipulating the mechanical reality of the device. That physical engagement (the weight and resistance of those dials) creates a psychological connection between the user and the process of image-making. It is the antithesis of the 'snapshot' mentality."

This trend isn’t limited to legacy heritage brands. Swedish design firm teenage engineering builds products that look like retro calculators but function as incredibly sophisticated synthesizers and samplers. Their iconic OP-1 and OP-Z units are defined by their small, brightly coloured, tactile knobs and buttons.

The joy of a teenage engineering product is entirely haptic. The interfaces are often abstract, forcing the user to learn through touch rather than visual prompts. It is designed to be performed with, like a musical instrument, rather than operated like a computer programme. A reviewer for The Verge described using the OP-Z as "pure haptic pleasure," noting that the physicality of the tiny, textured dials is where the creativity actually starts.

Even in the most functional of areas (the workspace) the tactile is returning. The mechanical keyboard community is exploding, filled with enthusiasts obsessive about the specific "actuation point," "bottom-out weight," and "thocky" sound profile of different keyboard switches. They are spending hundreds of dollars to replace the silent, frictionless membrane keyboards that came with their computers with something heavy, loud, and profoundly satisfying to use.

[V. Architecture] Tropical Brutalism

Concrete Jungle, Softened

A prime example of Tropical Brutalism, where heavy concrete forms frame lush, creeping vegetation. Source: Studio Saxe / Living ASEAN.

The word "Brutalism" often conjures images of massive, imposing Soviet block housing, or the grey, functionalist architecture of 1960s London. It is a style associated with the monumental, the functional, and the inherently cool. Concrete, its primary material, is often seen as cold, utilitarian, and oppressive when used in residential settings. However, a powerful architectural counter-movement is challenging this perception, redefining Brutalism not as an imposing urban force, but as an organic, domestic sanctuary. This is Tropical Brutalism.

From the lush jungles of Bali to the volcanic coastline of Mexico, architects are using raw, exposed concrete to build homes that are both fortress-like and inherently porous, integrating seamlessly with the vibrant natural landscapes that surround them.

Tropical Brutalism softens the material by emphasising its imperfect nature. The most crucial technique is "board-formed" concrete. Instead of being cast in smooth steel moulds, the liquid concrete is poured into rough wooden forms, allowing the texture and grain of the wood to be imprinted directly onto the setting material. This results in concrete that looks warm, textured, and even organic; more like petrified wood than cold stone.

"Concrete is the ultimate honest material," says Studio Saxe, an architecture firm known for its Tropical Brutalist residential work in Costa Rica. "But in a tropical environment, you have to create a dialogue. You can’t fight the environment; you must embrace it. We pair the heavy concrete structure with massive, open-air apertures, allowing the air to move freely and the greenery to invade. The concrete becomes a canvas for nature."

Architectural historian William J.R. Curtis, a specialist in Modernist architecture, argues that this tropical iteration is a refinement of Le Corbusier’s original vision. "It moves away from the 'unfriendly' Brutalism of urban hubs and returns to a kind of regional Modernism. It is a synthesis of local vernacular logic (the importance of ventilation, shading, and indigenous materials) with the structural expressive possibilities of reinforced concrete. It is the poetry of raw material, adapted for life in the sun."

[VI. Style & Fashion] Blueprint of Cool

The Eternal Rise of the Chore Coat

Paul Newman in the classic sun-bleached blue utility jacket in Cool Hand Luke (1967). Source: Warner Bros. / BAMF Style.

If the architecture of Tropical Brutalism is about concrete and nature, the architecture of the wardrobe is about denim and moleskin. The items that endure (those that earn their place through decades of service) are those born from the same utilitarian logic of the heavy board-formed wall or the ticking mechanical gear. They reject the ephemeral, the "frictionless" speed of fast fashion, in favour of something heavier, slower, and profoundly more satisfying.

The allure of the chore coat lies in its unpretentious authenticity. It was not designed to make a statement; it was designed to withstand a day of manual labour. Invented in late 19th-century France, its classic indigo-dyed moleskin or heavy cotton twill fabric was rugged. Its defining characteristics (three or four large patch pockets, a simple boxy fit, and a generic collar) were purely functional, designed to hold tools, pencils, and notebooks whilst allowing for a full range of motion.

This utilitarian purity is exactly what makes it so effortlessly cool. Cinema understands this implicit narrative. When Paul Newman wears a sun-bleached blue chore jacket in Cool Hand Luke, it's not a fashion choice; it's an extension of his anti-establishment, hardworking, "cool hand" character.

"Workwear always holds a fascination because it represents competence," says David Coggins, author of Men and Style. "It's clothing that says, 'I can fix things. I have a purpose.' When that functionality is translated into a civilian context, it becomes a shortcut to an effortless, capable aesthetic. The chore coat is great because it has no ego; it improves with wear, showing the patina of the user’s life."

This capable, no-ego aesthetic is exactly why the chore coat became the unofficial uniform of the modern "authentic" artist. You see it on musicians like Bill Callahan or Bon Iver’s Justin Vernon; artists known for their thoughtful, artisanal approach to music. For them, the chore coat signals a rejection of flashy, polished pop stardom. It says, "I am a craftsman, focused on my work."

Today, the chore coat has been adopted by high fashion, with luxury brands offering interpretations in cashmere, leather, and even silk. But the true blueprint (the heavy cotton indigo versions from heritage brands like Le Laboureur or Vétra) remains the archetype. The chore coat survives because it understands that some things (utility, authenticity, and durable construction) never go out of style. It is the blueprint of unpretentious cool.
